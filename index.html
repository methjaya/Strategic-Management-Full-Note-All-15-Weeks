WEEK 1

Introducing Strategy

What is Strategy?

Johnson et al (2008): The direction and scope of an organization over the long term, which achieves advantage in a changing environment through its configuration of resources and competences with the aim of fulfilling stakeholder expectations.

David (2009): Strategies are the means by which long-term objectives will be achieved.

Porter: A company's action plan for outperforming competitors and achieving superior profitability.

Characteristics of Strategic Decisions

  • Long-term direction of the organization
  • Scope of an organization's activities
  • Gaining advantage over competitors
  • Addressing changes in the business environment
  • Building on resources and competencies
  • Values and expectations of stakeholders

Three Tests for a Winning Strategy

  • Fit Test: Dynamic fit with external & internal situation
  • Competitive Advantage Test: Significant & sustainable advantage
  • Performance Test: Good profitability, financial strength, market standing

Levels of Strategy

Corporate Level

Overall direction & management of all businesses. Which industries/markets to compete in?

Business Level

Competitive & cooperative strategies at SBU level. How to beat rivals in this market?

Functional Level

Maximize resource productivity within each function. How to improve marketing efficiency?

The Four Strategy Lenses

🔵 Design Lens

Strategy as rational, analytical, systematic planning by top management. Deliberate.

🟢 Experience Lens

Strategy shaped by individual experience and organizational culture. History-driven.

🟣 Ideas Lens

Strategy emerges from new ideas — order and innovation from variety and diversity within/around organization.

🟠 Discourse Lens

Strategy language shapes understanding. Importance of being able to talk the language of strategy effectively.

Elements of Strategic Management

Strategic Position

Env. opportunities & threats; org strengths & weaknesses; basic purpose; how culture fits strategy.

Strategic Choices

Business, corporate & international strategies. Innovation, alliances, mergers.

Strategy in Action

Evaluating strategies (SAF), organizing for implementation, managing change.

Strategy lenses appear in MCQ. Design=rational/deliberate; Experience=culture-driven; Ideas=emergent/diversity; Discourse=language/communication.
WEEK 2

External Analysis: PEST & Five Forces

PEST Framework — General Environment Analysis

P — Political & Legal

  • Government regulations, trade policies
  • Political instability, corruption levels
  • Geo-political tensions, business legislation

E — Economic

  • GDP growth, inflation & price stability
  • Interest rates & monetary policy, fiscal policy
  • Trade environment, industrialization level

S — Social & Cultural

  • Demographic changes (age, population, gender)
  • Education, lifestyle trends, cultural values
  • Social mobility & career aspirations

T — Technological

  • Rate of change, R&D, AI/ML/IoT/blockchain
  • Digital platforms & ecosystems
  • Workforce tech skills & infrastructure

How to Use PEST (4 Steps)

  1. Identify key factors for change likely to impact strategy
  2. Identify alternatives within key factors
  3. Develop scenarios — matrix based on variability in key factors
  4. Explain the impact of each scenario on the organization

Scenario Planning: Select 2 key PEST factors with HIGH variability & HIGH impact. Plot them on X-Y axes to create a 2×2 matrix with 4 scenario quadrants. Name each scenario and explain the strategic response needed.

Porter's Five Forces Model

⬆THREAT OF NEW ENTRANTSBarriers: Scale, Access, Switching costs, Capital, Gov. policy
⬅BARGAINING POWER OF SUPPLIERSConcentrated, differentiated, forward integration threat
⚔
COMPETITIVE RIVALRY
Central force — determines industry attractiveness
BARGAINING POWER OF BUYERS➡Concentrated, low switching costs, backward integration
⬇THREAT OF SUBSTITUTESProducts offering similar benefit by different process

ATTRACTIVE Industry ✅

  • Rivalry is moderate
  • Entry barriers are high
  • Good substitutes do not exist
  • Suppliers & buyers in weak bargaining positions

UNATTRACTIVE Industry ❌

  • Rivalry is high
  • Entry barriers are low
  • Strong substitutes exist
  • Suppliers & buyers have considerable bargaining power

Five Forces: Strategic Implications

  • Positioning: Position company to better cope with current competitive forces
  • Anticipating: Exploit shifts in the forces
  • Shaping: Shape the balance of forces to create a more favorable industry structure

Strategic Groups

Organizations within an industry following similar strategies or competing on similar bases. Map them on a 2D grid using two differentiating characteristics. Useful for identifying direct competitors and positioning gaps.

For Five Forces essays: (1) Name all five forces, (2) Assess each as high/moderate/low with 2–3 reasons, (3) Give overall industry attractiveness verdict, (4) State strategic implications for the company.
WEEK 3

Internal Analysis: Resources, RBV & Value Chain

Resources

Tangible Resources

Assets that can be seen and quantified: buildings, machinery, cash, inventory.

Intangible Resources

Rooted in firm's history: brand equity, patents, reputation, culture, knowledge, ecosystem relationships. More sustainable competitive advantage — harder for competitors to imitate.

Capabilities

What the organization can DO with its resources. Built over time through complex interactions.

Threshold Capabilities

Minimum needed to compete and achieve parity with competitors. NOT a source of competitive advantage.

Core Competencies

Linked skills, activities & resources that: (1) deliver customer value, (2) differentiate from competitors, (3) can be extended and developed.

Dynamic Capabilities

Ability to renew and recreate strategic capabilities for changing environments. Three sub-processes: Sensing → Seizing → Reconfiguring.

Resource-Based View (RBV) — VRIO/VRIN Framework

CriterionQuestion to AskKey Details
V — ValuableDo resources/capabilities enable the firm to exploit opportunities or neutralize threats?Resources must add value for customers
R — RareHow many competitors already possess these capabilities?Resources need to be scarce relative to competitors
I — InimitableHow difficult or costly is it for others to imitate?Path dependence (historical), Causal ambiguity (reasons not fully known), Social complexity (interpersonal relationships)
O — OrganizedIs the firm organized to exploit the full competitive potential?Non-substitutable; cannot be replaced by other resources providing same value
Causal ambiguity = actions needed to create a capability are NOT fully known. Path dependency = historical events establish paths with lasting effects. Social complexity = complex interpersonal relationships. These 3 make resources INIMITABLE.

Value Chain Analysis (Porter)

SUPPORT ACTIVITIES

🏢 Firm Infrastructure (management, planning, finance, legal)👥 Human Resource Management (recruit, train, develop, compensate)
💡 Technology Development (R&D, product design, process equipment)🛒 Procurement (raw materials, fixed assets, advertising services)
📦 Inbound Logistics
Receiving, storing, inventory control
⚙️ Operations
Manufacturing, assembly, packaging
🚚 Outbound Logistics
Warehousing, order processing
📣 Marketing & Sales
Advertising, distribution, sales force
🔧 Service
Installation, repair, training

→ MARGIN = Total value created minus total costs of all activities = Competitive Advantage

Value Chain for Cost Leadership

  • Efficient inbound logistics (locate near suppliers)
  • Technology to reduce manufacturing cost
  • Low-cost transport for outbound logistics
  • Mass-scale marketing promotions, price to sell volume
  • Few management layers, AI-driven planning, cost-effective procurement

Value Chain for Differentiation

  • Superior R&D and product design
  • High quality inputs & procurement
  • Premium marketing and brand building
  • Exceptional customer service activities
  • Skilled HRM — hire and retain specialists

BCG Matrix (Portfolio Analysis)

⭐ STARS
High share · High growth
Invest & grow → future Cash Cows
❓ QUESTION MARKS
Low share · High growth
Invest selectively or divest
💰 CASH COWS
High share · Low growth
Harvest — fund Stars/QMs
🐕 DOGS
Low share · Low growth
Divest or harvest — exit

SWOT Analysis

Internal (Organization)

  • Strengths: Core competencies, brand, quality, resources
  • Weaknesses: Higher costs, weak innovation, narrow product line

External (Environment)

  • Opportunities: New markets, mergers, unserved segments
  • Threats: Slow growth, intense competition, powerful buyers/suppliers
Value chain exam questions: Draw the chain, then apply the specific strategy. Cost leadership = efficiency in ALL activities; Differentiation = focus on activities creating unique value for customers.
WEEK 4

Organization History, Culture & Strategic Drift

Corporate Culture (Thompson et al.)

The character of a company's internal work climate and personality — shaped by its core values, beliefs, business principles, traditions, ingrained behaviours and style of operating. Culture defines how a firm conducts business and interacts with key stakeholders.

Values

What is considered important; core beliefs and principles of the organization.

Norms

Define appropriate employee attitudes and behaviors; the unwritten rules.

Artifacts

Visible elements — office layout, dress code, ceremonies, logos, symbols.

Strong Culture ✅

  • Key values deeply held and widely shared
  • High agreement on what organization stands for
  • Builds cohesiveness, loyalty, commitment
  • Significant peer pressure to conform to norms
  • Example: Intel — "Speed" value widely shared

Weak Culture ❌

  • No consistent values preached or shared
  • Few behavioral norms visible in practices
  • Greater hostility to new ways of doing things
  • Employees view work as "just a place to work"
  • Little assistance in strategy implementation

Culture Promotes Strategy Execution

  • Provides clear guidance on behaviour that constitutes good performance
  • Produces peer pressure to conform to culturally accepted norms
  • Creates shared understanding across the organization

Culture as Barrier to Execution

  • Conflicts between old culture & new strategy behaviours
  • Cultural inertia resists change in dynamic environments
  • Entrenched "commonly held shared values" may oppose new direction

Strategic Drift (Johnson et al., 2008)

Definition: "The tendency for strategies to develop incrementally on the basis of historical and cultural influences but fail to keep pace with a changing environment."

PhaseDescriptionKey Characteristics
Phase I: Incremental ChangeLong periods of continuity; small strategy changesAligned with environment, success of past, experimenting within capability base
Phase II: Strategic DriftStrategy incremental but NO LONGER in line with environmentProblem of hindsight, building on familiar operations, rigidity in activities, lack of stakeholder support
Phase III: Period of FluxDownturn in performance; strategies change with no clear directionDisagreement: use historic capabilities or develop new ones?
Phase IV: Transformation or DeathMajor multiple changes to strategy or organizational failureChange in products, markets, capabilities, top management — or organization fails

Causes of Strategic Drift:

  • Path Dependency: "Early events and decisions establish policy paths that have lasting effects on subsequent events and decisions" (Johnson et al). Behavior rooted in the past becomes entrenched.
  • Cultural Inertia: Institutionalized norms, values, social networks resist change.

The Cultural Web (Johnson & Scholes)

Shows the behavioral, physical, and symbolic manifestations of a culture that inform and are informed by the taken-for-granted assumptions — the Paradigm.

📖 Stories

Narratives told by members about successes, disasters, heroes. Communicates what is important in the organization.

🎭 Rituals & Routines

Rituals: Activities highlighting importance (training, conferences). Routines: "The way we do things around here" day-to-day.

🏅 Symbols

Objects, events, acts that convey meaning beyond functional purpose (offices, cars, titles, logos, dress code).

💪 Power Structures

Most powerful groups associated with core beliefs. Powerful executives may sustain strategic drift.

🏗 Org. Structure

Reflects power — formal hierarchical structures emphasize that strategy is province of top management.

📊 Control Systems

Measurement and reward systems. What is monitored reveals culture (financial vs quality focus).

🎯 THE PARADIGM (Centre of the Web)

The taken-for-granted assumptions and beliefs. Collective experience applied to make sense of situations. Difficult for insiders to identify — outsiders often needed.

How to use the Cultural Web: (1) Analyze culture as it is NOW, (2) Analyze culture as org WANTS it to be, (3) Compare current vs desired, (4) Prioritize changes and develop a plan.

Cultural web exam: Identify each of the 6 elements + Paradigm and apply them to the case. For culture & strategy alignment: show how each element supports or conflicts with strategy execution.
WEEK 5

Strategic Purpose: Vision, Mission & Objectives

Hierarchy of Strategic Purpose
VISION
Where we want to go
MISSION
Reason for being / What we do
GOALS
Open-ended aspirations (no timeframe)
OBJECTIVES
Specific, measurable, time-bound targets (SMART)

Vision — Collins & Porras Framework

Core Ideology (Enduring Character)

  • Core Values: Essential, enduring tenets; timeless guiding principles requiring no external justification
  • Core Purpose: Organization's reason for being; captures the soul; not just about products or customers

Envisioned Future (Aspirations)

  • BHAG (Big, Hairy, Audacious Goal): Clear & compelling 10–30 year goal; catalyst for team spirit; clear finish line
  • Vivid Description: Vibrant, specific description of what it will be like to achieve the BHAG — translate vision into pictures

Vision Examples:

  • IKEA: "To create a better everyday life for the many people."
  • Nike: "To bring inspiration and innovation to every athlete in the world."
  • Google: "To provide access to the world's information in one click."

McKinsey's Horizon Model

Explains how organizations manage current business while building future opportunities. Defines the meaning of "long-term" strategy in practical terms.

HorizonTime FrameFocusExample
H1: Core Business0–3 yearsDefend & extend core business; sustain profitability; optimize operationsBank improving current savings products
H2: Emerging Opportunities3–5 yearsBuild emerging businesses that can become profitable in medium termBank launching mobile banking services
H3: Viable Future Options5–12+ yearsCreate genuinely new businesses for the future; explore options; highly uncertainBank exploring blockchain-based finance

Key insight: Successful organizations manage all three horizons simultaneously. H1 funds H2 and H3. H3 = the "long term" in strategy.

Mission Statement

"Declaration of an organization's reason for being." — What is our business?

Characteristics

  • Broad in scope (<250 words)
  • Inspiring and enduring
  • Identifies utility of firm's products
  • Reveals social & environmental responsibility
  • Includes nine standard components

Nine Components

  1. Customers — who do we serve?
  2. Products/Services — what do we offer?
  3. Geographic domain — where?
  4. Core technologies — what tech?
  5. Survival/Growth/Profitability — key accomplishments needed
  6. Company philosophy — distinctive philosophy?
  7. Self-concept — concept to convey?
  8. Desired public image — what image?
  9. Employee treatment — how are employees valued?

Objectives — SMART

  • Specific — Unambiguous in what is to be achieved
  • Measurable — Specified as a quantity
  • Attainable — Within realistic reach
  • Relevant — Appropriate to the mission
  • Time-bound — Has a completion date

Example: "To achieve 25% ROCE by December 2026"

Stakeholders & Governance (Freeman, 1984)

"Any group or individual who is affected by or can affect the achievement of an organization's objectives."

Includes: shareholders, employees, customers, suppliers, government, NGOs, communities, unions.

Stakeholder management: Balance all stakeholder expectations — not just shareholders — to ensure long-term firm success.

McKinsey Horizon Model is regularly asked. Remember: H1=now (0-3yr), H2=emerging (3-5yr), H3=future (5-12yr+). The "long term" in strategy refers to H3.
WEEK 6

Business Ethics & Corporate Social Responsibility

Business Ethics

The study of business situations, activities, and decisions where issues of right and wrong are addressed (Crane & Matten). Ethics derives from Greek 'ethos' — custom or habit belonging to character and virtues.

Deontology

Immanuel Kant. What rules, duties, or moral obligations apply? Right is right regardless of consequences.

Utilitarianism

Jeremy Bentham. What action produces the greatest good for the greatest number?

Virtue Ethics

Aristotle. What action aligns with good character and moral virtues?

Carroll's Pyramid of CSR

PHILANTHROPIC
Be a good corporate citizen
ETHICAL
Be ethical; do what is right and fair
LEGAL
Obey the law; fulfill legal requirements
ECONOMIC (FOUNDATION)
Be profitable; generate shareholder returns

Six Characteristics of CSR (Crane et al.)

1. Voluntary
Going beyond legal requirements
2. Internalizing Externalities
Managing negative social impacts
3. Multiple Stakeholder Orientation
Not just shareholders
4. Alignment of Social & Economic
NOT "economic and legal" — common exam trap!
5. Practice and Values
CSR in core day-to-day practices
6. Beyond Philanthropy
Integrate social concerns into business model
MCQ TRAP: "Aligning economic and legal responsibilities" is NOT a characteristic of CSR. The correct ones are: Voluntary, Beyond philanthropy, Multiple stakeholder orientation, Internalizing externalities, Alignment of social AND economic, Practice and values.

Strategic CSR (Porter & Kramer)

Creates a win-win situation where BOTH the business and stakeholders benefit. Goes beyond responsive CSR (good citizenship) to create shared value.

TypeDescriptionExample
Pure PhilanthropySocial benefit only; no business benefitDonating to charity with no strategic link
Responsive CSR (Good Citizenship)Good business practices; meet community obligationsEnvironmentally safe packaging
Strategic CSRCombined social AND economic benefit; integrated into core strategyDilmah MJF Tea Garden: addresses worker poverty AND improves productivity
Pure BusinessEconomic benefit only; no social considerationConventional profit-only activities

Four Pillars of Strategic CSR:

  • CSR perspective in strategic planning
  • Medium to long-term focus
  • Stakeholder perspective
  • Core operations integration (embedded in value chain)
WEEK 7

Business Level Strategy — Competitive Strategies

Strategic Business Unit (SBU): A part of an organization for which there is a distinct external market for goods or services that is different from another SBU. SBUs decentralize initiative, allow varying strategies, and encourage accountability.

Porter's Generic Competitive Strategies

Target / AdvantageLower CostDifferentiation
BROAD TARGET COST LEADERSHIP
Lowest cost among all competitors in the industry. Acceptable quality.		 DIFFERENTIATION
Unique attributes valued by broad range of buyers. Justifies premium price.
NARROW TARGET FOCUSED COST LEADERSHIP
Lowest cost within a specific niche segment.		 FOCUSED DIFFERENTIATION
Unique features for a specific niche segment. Premium pricing.

Middle ground = Best Cost Provider Strategy — combines differentiation AND low cost (hybrid approach)

Cost Leadership Strategy

Produce goods/services with acceptable features at the lowest cost relative to competitors (not necessarily absolute lowest).

Approach 1: Efficient value chain activities

  • Use online/social media marketing
  • Capture economies of scale
  • Learning and experience curve effects
  • Customer retention focus, AI adoption

Approach 2: Revamp value chain

  • Eliminate low-value-added steps (outsource)
  • Relocate facilities closer to markets
  • Offer basic, no-frills product/service
  • Use online technology (e.g. Pizza Hut)

Limitations:

  • Low-cost methods easily imitated by rivals
  • Technological breakthroughs open cost reductions for rivals
  • Ignores buyer interest in quality/features

Differentiation Strategy

Produce goods/services customers perceive as different in ways that are important to them, commanding a price premium.

Drivers of Differentiation:

  • Unique product features
  • Exceptional services
  • New technologies
  • Quality of inputs
  • Exceptional skills or experience
  • Sustainability initiatives
  • Ethical grounds (free of child labor, fair trade)

Differentiation in Value Chain:

  • Marketing, sales, customer service (brand image)
  • Product R&D and design (unique features)
  • Purchasing/procurement (quality inputs)
  • Production processes (technology)
  • Distribution (speed, reliability)

Focus Strategies

Targets a narrow segment, tailoring to that segment's specific needs exclusively.

Focused Cost Leadership:

IKEA — low cost for young style-conscious buyers (self-assembly, warehouse stores, no delivery, room settings).

Focused Differentiation:

Dilmah — premium ethical tea for environmentally conscious consumers; Kantala — sustainable vegan bags for niche market.

Success Factors for Focus:

  • Distinct customer needs (clear segment)
  • Distinct value chains (hard to imitate)
  • Viable segment economics (profitable size)

Best Cost Provider Strategy

Combines BOTH cost efficiency AND differentiation — "more value for money." For middle-market customers who want quality but are price-sensitive.

Southwest Airlines Example:

  • Cost: Single aircraft model (Boeing 737), secondary airports, no meals, 15-min turnaround, no reserved seats
  • Differentiation: High customer satisfaction, employee dedication, point-to-point service

The Strategy Clock (Johnson et al.)

Differentiation Zone

High perceived value. Commands price premium. Sustainable with brand loyalty.

Low-Price Zone

Lower price, lower perceived value. Compete on price. Cost efficiency critical.

Hybrid Strategy Zone

Moderate price AND moderate-high value. Sustainable if cost base allows.

Non-Competitive Zone ❌

High price, Low value. UNSUSTAINABLE — customers will not pay premium for inferior products.

Interactive Competitive Strategies

When a low-cost competitor enters your industry, available options for a differentiator:

  • Cannibalize bases of success — Remove organizational inertia
  • Undertake series of small moves — avoid big moves
  • Be unpredictable in responses
  • Use a cooperative strategy (ally with another player)
For case questions: Identify strategy (cost leadership/differentiation/focus) with justification from the case, then design the value chain showing HOW that strategy is delivered through specific activities.

Corporate Level Strategy & Strategic Growth Directions

Corporate level strategy is concerned with the overall purpose and scope of an organization and how value will be added to different parts (business units). Focus: scope of firm, resource allocation, long-term growth, synergy creation.

Ansoff's Matrix — Strategic Growth Directions

Products / Markets EXISTING Products NEW Products

Gain dominance in existing market. Lower prices, expand distribution, heavier promotions, attract rival's customers, stimulate product trial. Lowest risk. 		PRODUCT DEVELOPMENT

Related Diversification

Beyond current products/markets but within the capabilities of the value network. Core competence transferred across business units.

Creates value through:

  • Economies of scope — Cost savings from transferring capabilities
  • Operational relatedness — Sharing primary activities (e.g., shared distribution)
  • Corporate relatedness — Transferring management/technology knowledge across units

  • Efficient internal capital allocation
  • Purchasing and restructuring other companies

Main Strategic Alternatives

Limited Growth

  • Market penetration
  • Market development
  • Product development

Substantive Growth

  • Horizontal integration (acquire competitor)
  • Backward vertical integration (acquire input supplier)
  • Forward vertical integration (acquire distribution/retail)
  • Related diversification
  • Unrelated diversification

Retrenchment (Main Objective: SURVIVAL)

Used when declining profits due to recession, inefficiency, or competitor innovation.

  • Turnaround Strategy: Stabilize crisis → gain stakeholder support → define target markets → refocus & financial restructuring. New management often brought in.
  • Divestment: Sell a business unit to raise funds or eliminate poor strategic fit.
  • Cost/Asset Reduction: Cut unnecessary costs, sell assets.
  • Revenue Generation: Focus on profitable core strengths quickly.
Ansoff matrix is critical for case studies. When asked to "identify strategic growth directions," name the Ansoff quadrant + justify with evidence from the case. When asked for "future strategies," propose ones NOT yet used by the company. Always evaluate optimal using SAF.

Strategic Methods: Organic, M&A & Alliances

A strategic method is the means by which a strategy can be pursued.

Organic Growth (Internal Development)

Advantages:

  • Knowledge and learning accumulation (stays internal)
  • Spreading investment over time
  • No availability constraint
  • Strategic independence maintained
  • Culture management easier (no clash)

Challenges:

  • Must overcome entry barriers from scratch
  • Invest in new production capacity, supply chains
  • Hire, train employees, build distribution channels
  • Slower than M&A; higher financial risk

Acquisitions & Mergers

Motives for M&A:

  • Strategic: Extension (geography/products), Consolidation (remove competitors), Capabilities (acquire new ones)
  • Financial: Financial efficiency, Tax efficiency, Asset stripping/unbundling
  • Managerial: Personal ambition, Bandwagon effect

Common Problems in Acquisitions:

  • Integration difficulties (cultural fit is crucial)
  • Inadequate evaluation of target
  • Large/extraordinary debt
  • Inability to achieve expected synergy
  • Too much diversification

Target Choice Criteria:

  • Strategic fit: Target strengthens or complements the strategy
  • Organizational fit: Match in management practices, culture, staff characteristics

Two or more organizations share resources and activities to pursue a common strategy.

  • Critical to achieving an important objective
  • Helps build/sustain/enhance a core competency
  • Helps block a competitive threat
  • Opens important market opportunities
  • Mitigates a significant risk

  • Inability of partners to work well together
  • Changing conditions rendering purpose obsolete

WEEK 11

Innovation & Entrepreneurial Strategy

Invention vs Innovation

Invention: Conversion of new knowledge into a new product, process, or service.

Innovation: Invention PLUS putting the new product/process/service into actual use (commercializing it).

Disruptive Innovation

Innovations that radically change an industry's rules for producers and consumers. Examples: DVDs wiped out videotape; digital cameras eliminated film; smartphones disrupted multiple industries.

Four Innovation Dilemmas

DilemmaOption AOption BKey Insight
Technology Push vs Market PullTech Push: Knowledge from scientists drives innovationMarket Pull: Listen to users first; actual use drives innovationMost successful innovations balance both
Product vs Process InnovationProduct: New features of the final product (e.g. Apple design)Process: Improve how product is produced/distributed (e.g. Dell supply chain)Different competitive advantages from each
Open vs Closed InnovationClosed: Own internal resources; secretive; protect IPOpen: Deliberately import/export knowledge; exchange ideas openlyClosed=control; Open=speed and breadth
Tech vs Business Model InnovationTechnological: New science or technologyBusiness Model: Reorganize elements of business into new combinationsBusiness models as powerful as tech (Netflix, Airbnb, Uber)

Innovation Diffusion — The S-Curve

Diffusion = the process by which innovations spread among users. The S-curve reflects: initial slow adoption → rapid acceleration in diffusion → plateau representing the limit to demand.

Key S-Curve Concepts:

  • Tipping Point: Where demand suddenly takes off with explosive growth. Managers must invest BEFORE this to capture advantage.
  • Plateau: Eventual slowdown. Organizations should begin investing in the NEXT S-curve (H2/H3) before current growth peaks.

Speed of Diffusion Driven by:

  • Degree of performance improvement
  • Compatibility with existing factors
  • Complexity of product/marketing
  • Ability to experiment before purchase
  • Network effects — demand grows as more users adopt

First-Mover Advantages

  • Experience curve benefits
  • Scale benefits from being first
  • Pre-emption of scarce resources
  • Reputation and brand building
  • Buyer switching costs lock in customers

Late-Mover Advantages

  • Free-ride on first-mover's market development & R&D
  • Learn from first-mover's mistakes
  • Lower investment (no need to educate market)
  • Adopt improved technology; enter with more efficient processes

Entrepreneurial Relationships

Corporate Venturing

Investing externally in new ventures; protects ventures from internal bureaucracy and spreads risk.

Spin-offs / Spin-outs

Generation of small innovative units from larger organizations. E.g. Google spinning out Waymo.

Ecosystems

Fostering communities of suppliers, agents, distributors, franchisees, tech entrepreneurs. E.g. Apple App Store.

Stages of Entrepreneurial Growth:

Start-up (capital challenge) → Growth (management challenge) → Maturity (retaining enthusiasm; entrepreneurship → intrapreneurship) → Exit (succession, sale, IPO)

WEEK 12

Organizing for Strategic Success

Nature of Strategy Execution

"Execution is NOT a linear process — it is an ITERATIVE process." Technology, customer needs and competition don't adhere to any calendar.

Primary Challenges:

  • Failure to connect the dots (formulation ↔ execution gap)
  • Responding to continuous changes in tech, customer needs, competition
  • Allocating resources appropriately, reconciling conflicting demands
  • Balancing current operational needs with future businesses
  • Challenging the status quo

Structural Inertia

Resistance to change rooted in the size, complexity, and interdependence of organizational structures, systems, and formal processes.

Cultural Inertia

Institutionalized learning and common expectations manifest in the informal norms, values, social networks.

Organizational Configuration (Congruence Model)

Strategy, Structure, and Systems must be mutually reinforcing. McKinsey 7-S Framework: Strategy, Structure, Systems, Skills, Staff, Style, Shared Values — all 7 must align.

Hardware (Formal)

  • Critical Tasks: Important tasks for producing & marketing the product
  • Formal Organization: Structure (hierarchies, reporting lines), measurements, systems must support strategy

Software (People)

  • Skills & Competencies: Required to carry out critical tasks; skills acquisition, dynamic capability
  • Culture: "How we do things around here." Dysfunctional culture = root of many strategic failures

Organizational Structures

StructureDescriptionAdvantagesDisadvantages
FunctionalOrganized by function: production, sales, finance, HRClear responsibilities; specialists at senior level; simplified controlSenior managers overburdened; coordination problems; difficult with diversity
DivisionalSeparate divisions by product, service, or geographyFlexible; performance control by division; ownership of strategyDuplication; fragmentation; danger of losing central control
MatrixCombines dimensions simultaneously (e.g. product + geography)Integrates knowledge; flexible; good for global operationsSlow decisions; unclear responsibilities; high conflict
TransnationalGlobal network: knowledge sharing + specialization + coordinationCombines local responsiveness with global coordinationComplex to manage; sophisticated coordination required
Project-basedTeams created for projects, then dissolvedMaximum flexibility; teams match project needsLess stable; resource allocation challenges

Organizational Control Systems

1. Planning Systems

Direct control of INPUTS (resources consumed). Financial budgets, HR allocation, long-term investment planning.

2. Cultural Systems

Indirect control through norms and values (self-control). Recruitment (cultural fit), Socialization, Reward systems (pay, promotion, symbolic recognition).

3. Performance Targeting Systems (KPIs)

Focus on OUTPUTS of the organization. Key Performance Indicators, Balanced Scorecard, quality targets, profit/revenue targets.

4. Market Systems (Internal Markets)

SBUs compete for internal contracts/resources. Indirect control — SBUs must earn by being competitive within the organization.

The Balanced Scorecard (Kaplan & Norton)

💰 Financial Perspective

"How do we look to shareholders?"

KPIs: Revenue growth, profit margin, ROCE, shareholder value, EPS

👥 Customer Perspective

"How do customers see us?"

KPIs: Customer satisfaction score, market share, retention rate, acquisition rate

⚙️ Internal Business Processes

"What must we excel at?"

KPIs: Operational efficiency, quality defect rate, cycle time, innovation pipeline

🌱 Learning & Growth (Innovation)

"Can we continue to improve and create value?"

KPIs: Employee skills/training hours, staff satisfaction, R&D investment, system capabilities

STRATEGY MAP (Causal Link): Learning & Growth → enables better Internal Processes → improves Customer satisfaction → drives Financial performance
Balanced Scorecard is frequently asked. Remember the four perspectives and design measures that are LINKED (a strategy map). Traditional financial measures alone are insufficient for modern strategy.

StrategistRole & ContributionKey Risk/Limitation

Five Cognitive Biases in Strategic Decision Making

1. Confirmation Bias

Seeking out data that confirms a decision already made.

2. Anchoring Bias

Being tied to one piece of information in making a decision.

3. Saliency Bias

A particular analogy becomes unduly influential (salient).

4. Affect Bias

Managers become too emotionally attached to a particular option.

5. Risk Bias

Managers hold distorted views of risk (often over-optimistic).

Solution

Design a good DM process; involve diverse perspectives; challenge assumptions.

Strategy Methodologies

  • Strategy Workshops: Groups of executives, 1–2 days off-site; for formulation and reconsideration of strategies
  • Strategy Projects: Teams on specific strategic issues for a defined period. Need: clear mandate, top management commitment, milestones, appropriate resources
  • Hypothesis Testing: Start with a proposition and test with real-world data; used in strategy projects
  • Business Cases: Data and argument supporting a specific strategy proposal (e.g., new product investment)
  • Strategic Plans: Comprehensive plan for whole organization. Elements: mission, goals, environmental analysis, capability analysis, proposed strategy, resources, key changes

Open vs Closed Strategy

Open Strategy (more participants, more transparency):
  • Collective intelligence improves formulation
  • Increases implementation commitment and buy-in
  • Example: LEGO Ideas platform — customers propose product ideas
Risks of Open Strategy:
  • Too many participants slow decision-making
  • Risk of leaking sensitive information to competitors
WEEK 14

Evaluating Strategies

Post Hoc Evaluation

Evaluating a strategy already in operation. Evaluate outcomes against benchmarks. Role: allow corrective action. Tools: Balanced Scorecard, Margin Return Model.

Strategic Choice Evaluation

Techniques to decide which strategic option to choose from several alternatives. Primary tool: SAF Framework.

SAF Framework — Evaluating the Optimal Strategic Choice (Johnson & Scholes)

CriterionCore QuestionSub-questions & Tools
S — SUITABILITY Does the strategy address the key opportunities and constraints? Does it FIT?
  • Fits with environmental opportunities/threats (PEST, Five Forces)?
  • Builds on strategic capabilities (VRIO)?
  • Appropriate for stakeholder expectations and culture?
  • Tool: Ranking/Scoring matrix
A — ACCEPTABILITY Does the strategy meet stakeholder expectations? Is risk & return acceptable?
  • Return: ROCE, DCF analysis, expected financial benefits
  • Risk: Break-even, financial ratios, sensitivity analysis
  • Stakeholder reactions: Stakeholder mapping; political dimensions
F — FEASIBILITY Would the strategy work in practice? Can the org deliver it?
  • Can it be financed? (cash flow analysis, break-even)
  • Do the people/skills exist or can they be obtained?
  • Can required resources be obtained and integrated?
  • Tool: Resource deployment analysis, cash flow projections

Lynch's Evaluation Criteria (6 Criteria)

Consistency

In line with mission and objectives?

Suitability

Fits business and competitive environment?

Validity

Based on realistic assumptions?

Feasibility

Will it work? Are resources available?

Business Risk

Do returns justify the exposure?

Attractiveness to Stakeholders

Will key stakeholders support it?

Types of Control

Input Controls

Resources used in performance. Training hours, R&D budget allocated, financial inputs.

Behavior Controls

Activities that generate performance. Following procedures, sales calls made, compliance.

Output Controls

Actual performance results. Sales quotas, profit targets, customer satisfaction scores.

For 'determine the optimal strategic choice' questions: Evaluate each option using Suitability, Acceptability, and Feasibility. Create a clear table comparing options, then justify your recommendation clearly.
WEEK 15

Leadership & Managing Strategic Change

Types of Strategic Change (Balogun & Hope Hailey)

GRADUAL (Evolutionary speed)
BIG BANG (Revolutionary speed)
REALIGNMENT (Incremental extent)
ADAPTATION
Small step-by-step changes aligned with existing strategy. Most common and least risky.
RECONSTRUCTION
Rapid change without changing fundamental strategy. Turnaround situations. Cost-cutting without strategy change.
TRANSFORMATION (Fundamental extent)
EVOLUTION
Fundamental change implemented slowly over time through programs. Long-term cultural transformation.
REVOLUTION
Rapid and fundamental change of the whole business. Crisis-driven. Highest risk and disruption.

Diagnosing the Change Context

Context of Change

Assess: Time available, scope needed, diversity of stakeholders, capability for change, readiness for change.

Cultural Web

Diagnose current culture vs desired culture. Identify what needs to change in paradigm, stories, rituals, symbols, power, structure, controls.

Forcefield Analysis

Identify driving forces (FOR change) and restraining forces (AGAINST change). Strengthen drivers; weaken restrainers.

Transformational Leadership

  • Building a vision for the organization
  • Building org identity around collective values and beliefs
  • Energizing people to achieve the vision
  • Suitable for uncertain situations requiring fundamental change
  • Focus on 'soft' levers (culture, values, hearts and minds)

Transactional Leadership

  • Focus on hard levers of change (systems and controls)
  • Emphasize changes in structures, targets, financial incentives
  • Project management and monitoring performance
  • Adjusting style to context (situational leadership)

Styles of Leading Change (Spectrum)

StyleDescriptionBest When
DirectionLeader makes most decisions about what and how to change; uses authority to directCrisis; urgent change needed; low capability/willingness
ParticipationLeader retains coordination but delegates elements of the change processModerate capability; need some buy-in
CollaborationWidespread involvement in decisions about BOTH what AND how to changeHigh capability; need commitment and ownership
PersuasionGain support for change by generating understanding and commitmentChange already decided; need to build understanding

Lewin's Three-Step Change Model

① UNFREEZE

Present Stage

Overcome resistance & group conformity. Make driving forces stronger OR weaken/remove restraining forces. Create dissatisfaction with status quo. Question "why do we do it this way?"

→

② CHANGE (MOVE)

Transition Stage

Implement the change process. Get employees actively involved. Provide new behaviors, skills, and capabilities. Move from old state to new state.

→

③ REFREEZE

Desirable Stage

Stabilize the change. Balance driving and restraining forces. Introduce systems and conditions to reinforce and maintain new behaviors. Make change permanent.

Kotter's 8-Step Process for Leading Change

1Establish a sense of Urgency
2Create a guiding Coalition
3Develop a Vision & Strategy
4Communicate the change vision
5Empower broad-based action
6Generate short-term Wins
7Consolidate gains, produce more change
8Anchor new approaches in culture

Managing Change Programs

Program TypeWhen to UseKey Elements
Turnaround Strategy (Reconstruction)Crisis; rapid decline; company survival at riskChanging management, stabilizing crisis, gaining stakeholder support, defining target markets, refocusing & financial restructuring

EXAM PREP

Exam Question Answer Frameworks

Use these structured approaches for common question types. Always define key terms first and link every point to the case study.

A. Organizational Culture & Strategy Execution

1
Define culture: values, norms, artifacts. Mention cultural web elements (stories, rituals, symbols, power, structure, control, paradigm)
2
How culture PROMOTES execution: shared values, peer pressure to conform, clear guidance on behaviour, shared understanding
3
How culture can be a BARRIER: conflict between old culture and new strategy, cultural inertia, resistance to change in dynamic environments
4
Apply to case: Which cultural web elements are relevant? Are they aligned or misaligned with strategy? Give specific evidence from case.

B. Strategic Growth Directions (Ansoff + SAF)

1
Identify CURRENT directions: cite Ansoff quadrants with evidence from the case (e.g., "M&N entering India = market development")
2
Propose TWO OR THREE FUTURE directions (must be different from current; justify WHY each suits the company)
3
Apply SAF to choose the OPTIMAL one: which is most Suitable (environment + capabilities)? Acceptable (risk/return/stakeholders)? Feasible (resources)?
4
State recommendation clearly: "The optimal choice is X because it scores highest on Suitability (…), Acceptability (…) and Feasibility (…)"

C. Porter's Five Forces Industry Analysis

1
Choose an industry (or use the one in the case). Name and briefly define the Five Forces model.
2
For EACH of the 5 forces: Name it → Assess its strength (high/moderate/low) → Justify with 2–3 specific factors from that industry
3
Give overall verdict: Is the industry attractive or unattractive for earning profits? State clearly.
4
Identify strategic implications: What should companies in this industry do given this analysis? (positioning, anticipating, shaping)

D. RBV / Strategic Capabilities Assessment

1
Identify key resources and capabilities of the organization from the case (tangible and intangible)
2
Apply VRIO to each: Is it Valuable? Rare? Inimitable (how — path dep., causal ambiguity, social complexity)? Organized?
3
Classify: Threshold capability (meets parity) vs Core competence (competitive advantage)
4
Connect to competitive advantage: Which capabilities underpin the firm's strategic position?

E. Strategic Change Program

1
Diagnose context: What type of change is needed? (Adaptation/Evolution/Reconstruction/Revolution?) Use Balogun matrix.
2
Diagnose using Cultural Web and Forcefield Analysis: What are the driving and restraining forces?
3
Choose leadership style: Transformational or Transactional? Direction/Participation/Collaboration/Persuasion?
4
Describe the change program: Turnaround / Revolutionary / Evolutionary?
5
Apply Lewin's model: Unfreeze (how?) → Change (what actions?) → Refreeze (what systems to embed change?)

F. Value Chain Design for Specific Strategy

1
Cost Leadership Value Chain: Inbound (near suppliers, efficient inventory) → Operations (tech to reduce cost, org learning) → Outbound (low-cost transport, efficient order sizes) → Marketing (mass scale, price to sell volume) → Service (outsource, efficient installation) → Support: simplified management, cost-effective tech, procurement at lowest cost
2
Differentiation Value Chain: Identify WHERE uniqueness is created — R&D/design, premium procurement, skilled HRM, brand marketing, exceptional service — and justify how each justifies premium price

G. Balanced Scorecard Design + Strategy Map

1
Financial Perspective: Define 2–3 KPIs (e.g., revenue growth %, ROCE, profit margin, EPS)
2

Key Definitions — Use These in Your Answers

Always define key terms at the start of your answer. These are the exact definitions your examiners expect.

Strategy
The direction and scope of an organization over the long term, which achieves advantage in a changing environment through its configuration of resources and competences with the aim of fulfilling stakeholder expectations.
Johnson et al (2008)
Strategic Management
The art and science of formulating, implementing, and evaluating cross-functional decisions that enable an organization to achieve its objectives.
David (2009)
Competitive Advantage
When a firm creates greater value for users than the costs of supplying them AND that value is superior to that of rival SBUs.
Core Competence
A linked set of skills, activities and resources that together: (1) deliver customer value, (2) differentiate from competitors, and (3) can be extended and developed.
Dynamic Capability
Organization's ability to renew and recreate its strategic capabilities to meet the needs of changing environments. (Sensing, Seizing, Reconfiguring)
Strategic Drift
The tendency for strategies to develop incrementally on the basis of historical and cultural influences but fail to keep pace with a changing environment.
Johnson et al (2008)
Path Dependency
Where early events and decisions establish policy paths that have lasting effects on subsequent events and decisions.
Johnson et al (2008)
Cultural Web
Shows the behavioral, physical, and symbolic manifestations of a culture that inform and are informed by the taken-for-granted assumptions (paradigm).
CSR (Carroll, 1979)
The economic, legal, ethical, and discretionary (philanthropic) expectations that society has of organizations at a given point in time.
Strategic Alliance
Where two or more organizations share resources and activities to pursue a common strategy.
Emergent Strategy
Strategy that develops incrementally through actions, learning, and responses to the environment; not fully planned in advance.
Deliberate Strategy
A planned, intentional strategy developed systematically through formal analysis and rational decision-making before implementation.
Spin-offs / Spin-outs
The generation of small innovative units from larger organizations.
Corporate Venturing
Investing externally in new ventures, thereby protecting early-stage ventures from internal bureaucracy and spreading risk.
Strategic Group
Organizations within an industry or sector with similar strategic characteristics, following similar strategies or competing on similar bases.
Market Sensing
The processes developed to collect and analyze market information to learn about customers, competitors, and channel members.
Cultural Inertia
Institutionalized learning and common expectations manifest in the informal norms, values, and social networks that resist organizational change.
Structural Inertia
Resistance to change rooted in the size, complexity, and interdependence in the organization's structures, systems, and formal processes.

Final Exam Advice — 3-Hour Strategy for Success

Part A — MCQ (10 marks, ~15 min)

  • Read ALL options carefully before choosing
  • Watch for "NOT" questions — very common trap
  • Use elimination: cross out obviously wrong options
  • Common traps: CSR characteristics, strategy lens definitions, types of inertia, drift phases
  • If unsure, eliminate 2 wrong options first, then choose

Part B — Case Study (~24 marks, ~70 min)

  • Read the case TWICE before answering any question
  • Underline key facts, company actions, environmental changes
  • Link every answer back to the case with specific evidence
  • Use frameworks by NAME (value chain, cultural web, Ansoff, SAF)
  • Draw diagrams where possible (Ansoff, value chain, BSC)
  • Allocate time per mark: ~3 min per mark

Part C — Essays (choose 2 of 3, ~13 marks each, ~35 min each)

  • Read all 3 questions FIRST, then choose the 2 you know best
  • Structure your answer BEFORE writing: intro → body → conclusion
  • Use theory + application + local examples
  • Include definitions, models, and real Sri Lankan business examples
  • Never leave a Part C question blank — partial answers get partial marks

⭐ Golden Rules

  • Always define key terms at the start of each answer
  • For models, DRAW THEM when possible (Ansoff, value chain, BSC, Five Forces)
  • Connect answers to Sri Lankan examples from the case AND your own knowledge
  • Theory alone = 60%. Theory + application + examples = 90%+
  • Time allocation: MCQ 15 min · Part B 70 min · Part C 70 min (35 each) · review 5 min
